Autopsy Report — Rustem Vladimirovich Slobodin
Act No. 5. Examination conducted March 8, 1959, at the Regional Bureau of Forensic Medicine. The body of Slobodin Rustem Vladimirovich, age twenty‑three, was presented for post‑mortem examination following recovery from mountainous terrain under winter conditions. The deceased was reportedly part of a hiking expedition whose members perished under undetermined circumstances.

External inspection revealed a young adult male of athletic build, found in a partially flexed posture. The upper extremities were drawn inward, fingers contracted, suggesting terminal muscular rigidity or collapse during attempted locomotion. The lower limbs were asymmetrically bent. Body was received with insulated [[ski pants]] fastened with a button and a belt. In the pockets are a pack of matches with 43 match sticks, a pocket knife on a long string, a comb in a case, two pieces of string, a pencil, and a cotton sock. Such redistribution of garments may indicate environmental stress or disorientation.

The skin of the face is a blue-red color. Postmortem lividity is present with blue-red spots abundantly located on the posterior surface of the [[neck]], [[torso]] and extremities. Rigor mortis has resolved in the muscle groups of the joints. The skin of the upper limbs down to the [[wrist]] joints, as well as the lower limbs, are of bluish-pink color. The soft tissue of the surface of the back and palms of the hands are brown-purple in color. In the area of the wrist joint of the hand there is a graze wound protruding from the soft tissue in an area 8 x 1.5 cm in size covered in dry parchment-density crust under the layer of skin. Above them are two scoring marks that are linear in shape under dry brown crust with a length of up to 1.5 cm located parallel to and about 0.3 cm from each other. The eyebrows are blond and bushy. The eyes are partially open. On the upper right eyelid there is an abrasion that is brown-red in color with a size of 1 x 0.5 cm. No patterned imprint or clearly demarcated weapon signature was identified.

The skin flaps of the [[skull]] are moist, juicy and shiny. In the corresponding right lobular area and left and right lobular muscles there is a spilling hemorrhage into the soft tissue. There is a deep bone fissure from the left temporal bone along the direction of the upper forward area of the lobular bone with separation of 0.3 cm and a length of up to 6 cm. The fracture is 1.5 cm from the sagittal suture. The dura mater is bluish in color, and its vessels are slightly filled with blood. The brain matter appears as an unformed mass that is green red in color with undifferentiated contours of the ventricles, as well as the white and grey matter. In the upper area of the left pyramid there is hemorrhaging under the left bone plate with a size of 0.3 x 0.4 cm. Despite the upper fissure, the bones of the skull base are whole.

The right [[leg]] is bent at the pelvis at an angle of 120 degrees and at the [[knee]] at a right angle. The left leg is bent at the pelvis at a right angle and at the knee at an angle of 60 degrees. Both legs are touching each other in the area of the internal surface of the lower area of the thigh and the upper third of the [[shin]]. The valves of the heart, aorta and pulmonary arteries are smooth, thin and cloudy dark red color. The coronary vessels of the heart are freely passable. The internal surface of the aorta is smooth and clean. The surface of the lungs is blue-red in color and dough-like when palpated. When sectioned the lung tissue is dark red in color. When pressed, a large amount of foamy bloody liquid and dark liquid blood is released from the surface. The lumen of the larynx and bronchi are free. The mucosa of the esophagus, trachea and bronchi are pink-red in color. The stomach contained about 100 cm3 of a brown-yellow mucous mass. The mucosa of the stomach is turgid and grey-red in color with the good expression of the folds.

Conclusion: based on the data of the examination of the body of Slobodin Rustem Vladimirovich, 23 years old, and considering the circumstances of the case, I believe that the death of Slobodin was caused by the action of low temperature/freezing, which is evidenced by the swelling of the meninges, the blood-filled internal organs, the spots on the gastric mucosa, and the third- and fourth-degree freezing of the fingers. The fracture noted above could have occurred during a fall by Slobodin or the impact of a hard object such as rocks, ice, etc. A blunt object could have caused the above-mentioned trauma. When this happened it would have caused Slobodin to become stunned and allowed for his rapid freezing. The absence of explicit bleeding under the meninges allows for the assumption that Slobodin’s death came as a result of his freezing. The damage was caused during life, as well as in the agonal state and post mortem. The data of the examination of Slobodin’s body allows for the presumption that his last meal was 6-8 hours before the time of death. The presence of alcohol was not detected during the examination.

Autopsy Report — Yuri Nikolaevich Doroshenko
Act No. 2. Examination conducted March 4, 1959. Body of Doroshenko Yuri Nikolaevich, age twenty‑one, recovered from a forested area near the expedition campsite.
The body under examination is a male body of regular build. The body position is as follows: the upper limbs are drawn up at the shoulder joints towards the head and are bent at the [[elbow]] joints. The finger joints are also bent. The lower limbs are stretched. The head is turned to the left. The deceased was clothed in partial garments at the time of recovery, though examination of layering revealed the conspicuous absence of outer insulating articles. Despite prevailing sub-zero conditions, the body was effectively [[only in underwear]] with minimal additional textile coverage, limited to thin underlayers and damaged hosiery. On the inside of the pants, there is an identification tag reading 5П1513.  and areas of thermal alteration.

The length of the body is 180 cm. The body has a good nutritional status and well-developed muscle groups. Crimson purple post-mortem spots are located on the back of the neck, torso and extremities. Rigor mortis has resolved in the muscle groups of the joints. There is dark 6-cm-long chestnut hair on the head, cut in a crew-cut style. In the temporoparietal and occipital zones, there is an area where the ends of the hair are burnt, the hair is dirty with moss remains and fir needles, the skin of the face is brown-purple color, the contour of the face is smoothed. The eyebrows are dark chestnut and thick, the eyes are closed, the corneas are cloudy, the irises are brown, the pupils are enlarged, and the eyelid mucosa is pale pink.

Internal inspection demonstrated intact cranial bones. The soft tissue in the [[toes]] is dark purple. Examination by touch shows that the skeleton bones and cartilages are intact. Pulmonary sections exuded foamy, blood‑tinged fluid under compression. The base of the brain vessels is regular. The subcutaneous fat of the body is well-developed. The position of the internal organs is normal. The pleural cavities are empty.

Conclusion: according to the examination results of the body belonging to Doroshenko Yuri Nikolayevich, aged 21, and considering the circumstances of the case, we believe that the death occurred as the result of low temperature exposure (freezing), which can be proved by the following evidence: meninges oedema, strong hyperaemia in the internal organs, cardiac cavities full of liquid dark blood, petechiae in the gastric mucosa (known as Wischnewsky spots), an excessively full bladder, along with the third and fourth degree frostbite of fingers and toes. External examination makes this more likely as the body was retrieved [[only in underwear]]. External damage was found, such as multiple abrasions and skin wounds caused by a blunt object that could occur as the result of falling or bruising against stones, ice, and other objects. The injuries mentioned above occurred while alive, during the agonal state, and after death. The injuries mentioned above can be described as slight injuries without damage to health. This examination of the body gives reasons to state that the last meal was consumed 6 to 8 hours before death. Alcohol was not found during the tests.

Autopsy Report — Georgiy Alekseevich Krivonischenko
Act No. 3. Examination conducted March 4, 1959. Body recovered in proximity to forest edge. Decedent aged twenty-three.
The body under examination is a male body of regular constitution and satisfactory nutrition. The head is turned slightly to the left. The right arm is bent upward at the elbow and the fingers are drawn inward toward the head. The left arm is flexed at the elbow and rests across the torso. The right leg is extended, while the left leg is bent at the [[hip]] and slightly at the [[knee]].
The deceased was clothed in partial garments at the time of recovery. The majority of insulating outer layers were absent. Despite winter conditions, the body was effectively [[only in underwear]]. The absence of protective clothing in these conditions is notable.

On the left lower [[leg]] there is a large area of tissue damage extending from the lower third of the [[shin]] upward. The skin in this region is brown-black in color, dry, and firm to the touch, with areas of bursting and separation of the outer layers. The surface appearance is consistent with exposure to high temperature. The rear of the left foot shows similar darkened areas. The distal phalanges of the fingers and [[toes]] are dark purple, shriveled, and solid when palpated, consistent with advanced freezing.

Upon reflection of the scalp, diffuse hemorrhage is noted in the right temporal region. The bones of the [[skull]] are intact. The brain matter appears softened and poorly differentiated. The lungs are dark red in color and release foamy, blood-tinged liquid when sectioned and compressed. The cardiac cavities contain dark fluid blood. The gastric mucosa shows numerous small hemorrhages on the folds. The bladder is markedly distended.

Conclusion: Based on the examination of the body of Krivonischenko Georgiy Alekseevich, aged 23, and taking into account the circumstances of the case, death occurred as a result of low temperature exposure (freezing), as evidenced by organ congestion, petechial hemorrhages of the gastric mucosa, and severe frost injury of the extremities. The burn of the left lower limb is consistent with exposure to open flame and appears localized. External injuries in the form of abrasions and grazes may have resulted from falls or contact with stones, ice, or other hard surfaces. These injuries were sustained during life, in the agonal state, or post mortem. No alcohol was detected.
Combined Autopsy Report - Ravine recovery

Examination conducted May 9–10, 1959.
Bodies recovered May 4, 1959 from a ravine approximately 75 meters from the cedar tree, beneath a snow layer measuring up to four meters in depth. The bodies were located in a running stream bed. The deceased were identified as: Lyudmila Aleksandrovna [[Dubinina]], age 20, Semyon (Alexander) Zolotaryov, age 38, Alexander Kolevatov, age 24, Nikolai [[Thibeaux-Brignolles]], age 23. The bodies were found in proximity to one another in the ravine channel, partially submerged in flowing water.

General Observations: All four bodies were dressed in multiple layers of clothing at the time of recovery. It was established during inspection that several garments did not correspond to the original owners, and certain articles previously documented on other deceased members were now present on these bodies. The clothing therefore included items belonging to other members of the group; effectively [[the clothes of the previous bodies]].
Several garments were removed for laboratory testing. Subsequent analysis revealed measurable contamination of some clothing items with elevated levels of beta [[radiation]]. The degree of contamination varied between samples and was not uniformly distributed. For the purposes of record, the garments were described as [[radioactive]]. All four bodies displayed post-mortem changes consistent with prolonged exposure to water and burial beneath snow.

Lyudmila Dubinina: External Examination. The body of Dubinina was found lying prone in the stream bed. She was clothed in layered garments, including [[sweaters]] and torn trousers, some belonging to other members of the group. The lower garments showed signs of water saturation and tearing. The soft tissues of the face were largely absent. In the upper region of the face and nasal bridge, soft tissue was missing, exposing the underlying facial bones. Both eye sockets were empty; the eyeballs were absent. 
The upper lip on the right side was partially destroyed with exposure of the upper jaw. The oral cavity examination revealed absence of the [[tongue]]. The floor of the mouth and diaphragm of the oral cavity were also absent. The jaw bone demonstrated unusual mobility. Upon internal inspection, multiple bilateral fractures of the [[ribs]] were identified, with associated hemorrhage into surrounding musculature.

Semyon Zolotaryov: External Examination. The body of Zolotaryov was discovered supine in the ravine. He was wearing layered clothing, including items not originally assigned to him in earlier inventories. Several of these garments were also later identified as containing [[radioactive]] contamination. The face demonstrated extensive soft tissue destruction. Both [[ears]] were missing. The surrounding tissue appeared degraded. On palpation of the chest, abnormal mobility of the ribs was detected. Subsequent internal examination revealed multiple rib fractures and hemorrhage into the thoracic cavity.

Alexander Kolevatov: External Examination. Kolevatov was located adjacent to the others within the stream channel. He was dressed in layered garments including outerwear not originally belonging to him. The face exhibited moderate soft tissue loss. The nasal cartilage was flattened. The skin in the region of the forehead and brow showed absence of hair and tissue, including the loss of the [[eyebrows]]. The eyes were partially degraded. No massive skeletal fractures were detected upon preliminary palpation. The chest cavity did not demonstrate the same degree of structural instability as seen in Dubinina or Zolotaryov.

Conclusions: Dubinina and Zolotaryov sustained severe closed chest trauma resulting in internal hemorrhage. Thibeaux-Brignolles died from cranial trauma. Kolevatov demonstrated no lethal skeletal injury; death is attributed to freezing. The soft tissue destruction of the facial regions (including the absence of the tongue, eyeballs, and eyebrows from some individuals)  is unattributed. Changes possibly to post-mortem changes, such as consumption by [[predators]]. The redistribution of garments among the deceased indicates removal and transfer of clothing prior to death.
