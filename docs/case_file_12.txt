Case File 12 — Protocol of Inspection of Items Recovered at Scene
Ivdel. 5–7 March 1959.

Junior Counselor of Justice Ivanov, acting in accordance with Article 78 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, conducted a formal inspection of material evidence recovered from the vicinity of the tent site of the deceased tourist group, as well as from the forested area containing traces of a fire and the bodies later identified as Krivonischenko and Doroshenko. The inspection was carried out in the presence of representatives of the Ural Polytechnic Institute and members of the press. Among those present for purposes of identification was fourth-year student of UPI, [[Yuri Yudin]], who had separated from the expedition earlier due to illness and was therefore considered capable of recognizing group property based on personal familiarity, diary entries, and shared preparation of equipment.
The tent and surrounding equipment were found partially collapsed and covered with windblown snow. No conclusive evidence of structural compression by [[avalanche]] mass, [[snow slab]], or [[snow drift]] displacement was recorded at the time of inspection, although snow accumulation patterns were noted.


1. Property of Yuri Yudin
A black backpack of worn construction with a repaired side seam contained personal hygiene items, letters addressed to Kolmogorova, medical supplies including bandages and iodine, powdered milk tins repurposed for medicaments, and a flashlight. According to [[Yuri Yudin]], certain medical articles within the pack had been transferred to Kolmogorova at the Second Northern settlement when he departed the group. Garments included quilted outerwear, multiple pairs of woolen and vigogne socks, mended tent material, mittens of varying weave, and decorative coverings fashioned from tarpaulin fabric. Additional clothing layers found on the body corresponded with autopsy documentation and demonstrated redistribution of insulation layers.


2. Property Attributed to Igor Dyatlov
Recovered articles included a fur vest, tarpaulin outer garments, woolen hat, coarse mittens, and ski equipment. Within the pockets were located a notebook, compass, pocketknife secured by cord, rope segments, wire, garlic cloves, and personal eyewear in a grey case. Notably, a [[fur vest]] discovered on Dyatlov’s body was stated by [[Yuri Yudin]] to have originally belonged to him and to have been given to Kolevatov on 28 January 1959, suggesting subsequent exchange among participants. Such exchanges complicate strict attribution of layered clothing. Additional red-stained [[fabric]], a torn [[strap]], and a broken [[frame]] possibly belonging to eyewear were documented but not conclusively linked to a particular owner.

3. Property Attributed to Lyudmila Dubinina
A black backpack containing toiletries, broken spectacles, letters, elastic bandage, writing instruments, and a hiking badge pinned above the pocket was identified as Dubinina’s. Clothing layers included ski trousers, balaclava, felt boots, and multiple sock types. [[Yuri Yudin]] stated he had personally given her grey wool socks prior to his departure. The repetition of redistributed garments among members requires consideration in any analysis of final clothing configuration. Items located near the ravine area included additional torn [[textile]], fragments of [[insulation]], and distorted metal fittings. These materials were not immediately identifiable as belonging to any one member.

5. Property Attributed to Rustem Slobodin
A khaki backpack marked with a small rubber toy bear contained binoculars, camera equipment, repair tools, notebooks, and sewing materials. On the body were discovered multiple clothing layers including sweater, checkered shirt, insoles placed between garments, and four pairs of socks on one foot while the corresponding felt boot was absent. The asymmetry of footwear distribution remains unexplained in this protocol. Possible causes such as redistribution among participants, loss during movement, or removal under conditions of [[panic]] or [[disorientation]] cannot be assessed within this document.

Unassigned items found at the scene: 
Numerous items remained without confirmed ownership, including torn boot covers (nine pairs), cotton and wool socks in various states of wear, gloves, balaclavas, repair materials, spectacles. Among miscellaneous items near the tent were scraps of unidentified [[cloth and glasses]] fragments, though attribution to a specific individual could not be immediately established. [[Yuri Yudin]] stated on the record that he had seen [[United States Military]] officials in Ivdel, the town from which they departed, wearing similarly marked items. This protocol concerns identification only and does not draw conclusions regarding mechanism of injury, redistribution of clothing at time of death, or sequence of events. It remains evident that prior exchanges of garments, shared medical supplies, and communal storage of equipment introduce ambiguity into later forensic interpretation.
